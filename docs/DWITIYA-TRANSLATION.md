## Adhyāy 65: The Origin of Hingulā (હિંગુળા ઉત્પત્તિ)
*Printed pp. 205–208 (PDF 211–214)*

*Sūta said:* I bow to the Goddess **Hingulā**, endowed with all perfections (sarva-siddhi), self-manifest, dwelling upon the mountain, the abode of all the gods.

*Sūta-Purāṇi said:* I make obeisance to Goddess Hingulā, giver of every desired thing, who by her own majesty destroyed all the demons. This Hingulā dwelt upon a great mountain in the **Sindhu** country, and men came to call that great tīrtha by the name "Unequalled" (Anuttama), the "Peerless" (Advitīya).

[The sages said:] "O Sūta, best of those who dispel our doubts, tell us the Māhātmya of Goddess Hingulā, in brief and in full. Who first worshipped her, and to what end? In what manner is she ever to be worshipped? How did Hingulā come to be? ... Tell us all of it; for Brahmā brought you forth to show [mortals] the far shore of the world."

*Sūta-Purāṇi said:* Now I shall tell you the origin of Hingulā, by the mere hearing of which one gains a majesty like the Goddess's own. Brahmā, Vishnu, Rudra and the other great gods do not [fully] know her power; how then could men know it? She is Māyā, the mother of the universe; in her the whole world abides. Though she is **nirguṇa** (without attributes), by her own will she can become **saguṇa** (with form). Who is able to know her power and her arising? Yet I shall tell you her origin.

*Sūta said:* In ancient times, in the country of **Rāmbhaka**, there was a rākṣasa named **Viprachitti**. He had two sons, **Hingul** and **Sindur**. The two of them conquered all the gods, even Vishnu. At this the gods, stricken, took refuge with **Pārvatī**. Pārvatī, knowing their distress, brought forth a son from [the dust of] her own body, armed him with every weapon, and sent him to do battle against the demon **Sindurāsura**. The demon summoned that son of Pārvatī to combat. Sindurāsura, with all his weapons and accompanied by other demons bearing weapons of many kinds, stood ready for battle; and a thrilling (romāñchajanaka) battle took place between them. By Pārvatī's grace, Sindurāsura was slain at a single stroke. At this all the other demons fled, and went each to his own abode. The Goddess's son, [great in valour], came in supreme joy to the Goddess and joyfully recounted the whole event to his mother. To that son the Mother gave the title **Gaṇapati** (lord of the hosts).

[The sages asked:] "After the demon Sindur was slain, what of Hingul? Who killed Hingul? Tell us all of it in full."

*Sūta-Purāṇi said:* When Sindur was slain in battle, his younger brother **Hingul** was wandering in **Pātāla** near **Hāṭakeshwar**, disporting himself in various pleasures. When the defeated demons came to Pātāla, Hingul asked them for news of his brother. They said: "Your elder brother has been slain by the Goddess's son, and we, defeated in battle, have fled here." Hearing that the brother of "valiant Hingul" had been killed, the demon was greatly grieved: "[Where now is] my valour? What shall I do now?"

"I shall perform tapas to destroy the gods." Resolving thus, he went to **Mount Meru**; raising his arms on high and standing [upon the tip of his toe] upon the earth, he began a **tapas of a thousand years**. Hingul's great austerity so terrified the gods that, trembling, they took refuge with **Brahmā**. The gods said: "This demon performs a fierce tapas. Will he, with the very earth, consume us all like fire?" Hearing this, Brahmā said: "To accomplish the gods' purpose, I shall bring him down from his tapas there on Sumeru." Reassured, the gods returned to their hermitages. Brahmā, with **Nārada** and the others, came to Mount Sumeru. The demon rose, and with bowed head looked upon Brahmā with reverence; making obeisance, doing worship, and folding his hands, he stood. When Brahmā said "Ask a boon," he took water from his **kamaṇḍalu** and sprinkled it [upon him], saying: "Ask whatever is in your mind; pleased with you, I am ready to grant all." Greatly delighted, the demon spoke thus: "O Brahmā, may I not be slain by any being created on earth or in Pātāla, nor by any weapon; and let no one among the gods be able to stand against me. Grant me these two boons." Brahmā granted him the boon by the **bīja-mantra** and departed.

Then the demon went down to Pātāla, [gathered] the demons, and rose up onto the earth. Hingul told all the demons [to make ready]: "Now [I shall subdue Pārvatī and] keep the gods in submission; today we shall conquer the gods of Swarga." Resolving thus, all the demons, hungry for dominion, set out for battle. Indra too heard of their enterprise in the heavens. All the gods began to tremble and to flee their stations; as winds they hid before Hingul, and, bound by fear, took to dwelling in their secret places.

[**Hingulāsura took possession of Swarga**, and the gods, driven out, fled. Once more they took refuge with **Pārvatī** and pleased the Goddess with hymns; and the Goddess, [to draw the demon to his doom], assumed an **enchanting (manohar) form**.]

*Thus ends the sixty-fifth chapter, "The Origin of Hingulā", of the Shrī Hingulapurāṇa within the Skanda Purāṇa.*

## Adhyāy 66: The Origin of Hingulā, continued
*Printed pp. 208–212 (PDF 214–218)*

[Drawn by the Goddess's enchanting form, the demon Hingulāsura, who was sporting with apsaras, beheld the **Jagadambikā** and, abandoning the apsaras, came toward her.]

Smitten, the demon spoke sweet words. **Hingulāsura said:** "O lotus-eyed one, who are you, and whence have you come, and what do you desire? Whose [bride] are you upon this earth? Nowhere on earth or in heaven have I seen beauty like yours. Have the gods abandoned you? I am utterly bewitched by your loveliness, come, sport with me; the mere sight of your form robs me of my wits." Thus, paying her homage, the demon spoke much to the Goddess. But the Goddess, giving him no answer, passed out [of the place] and began to spread across the earth; and the demon followed close behind. Wandering thus, the demon came to the **Sindhu ocean**.

There the Goddess said to him: "Why do you follow me, roaming across the earth? By the wandering of a demon like you, my company is made hard to attain, stand off at a distance. Is there anyone able to accomplish the [great] work I have begun? ... O chief of demons, go about your own business." The demon-king answered: "Whatever your work may be, tell me; though my very life should perish, I shall do it. I am your servant." And bound by enchantment (**kāmaṇ**), the demon followed after her still.

In the Sindhu land the demon took on a tangible, gross form, dark as a storm-cloud, gleaming with weapons. The Goddess reflected: *No god, yakṣa, man or nāga can bring about this demon's death; therefore [I must draw him into me].* Considering this, the Goddess put forth a form; the demon, beholding it, came on. **Hingulā said:** "To the very depths you have followed me; now therefore I shall be your lord, and my work is at hand." So saying [she drew him on], and the demon came close to the Goddess, and in a moment he was **absorbed into the Goddess's body**. As he entered her, crying *"Devī!"*, he fell to the ground; his life-breath departed, and **he attained supreme welfare (param kalyāṇ, liberation)**.

Then his released soul began to praise the Goddess. **The demon said:** "O Goddess, lord of gods and demons, I bow to you. I am dissolved into your body, protect me, for you are the primordial Shakti (Ādyā-Shakti) of all. Beholding your form I was deluded; by my own sin [I am undone]. Of you I ask a boon." **The Goddess said:** "O Hingulāsura, I am pleased by your praise; ask what you will." In sweet speech the demon said: "Since I have been the occasion [of your manifestation here], I ask only this: by my name and form let [this place] endure upon the earth to the end of the kalpa; and whoever comes to you, let all their desires be fulfilled."

The demon gave up his life, and the Goddess was made [serene]. Brahmā, Vishnu and all the gods began to praise her.

**The gods said:** "O Mother! You are the parent of the whole world; we have come to your refuge; you are the remover of all evils. But in granting this asura a boon you have not done well." When the gods had thus praised her, the Goddess said: "O Brahmā, I have granted him no [true] boon. To give a boon to one of cruel nature is like feeding nectar to a serpent. I have **slain** this demon; yet through the fame of my name he shall endure in the world. **From the name 'Hingulāsura,' my name shall be called Hingulā Devī.**" Saying this, before the eyes of all the gods, Goddess Hingulā **vanished (antardhāna)**. And thereafter the chief among the tīrthas of the **Sindhu** country came to be known by the name of **Hingulā**; the region where [the demon] vanished became most abundantly fruitful. Great is the Māhātmya of these tīrthas, which **Nārada** first heard, and which the sage **Nārāyaṇa** told to Nārada at length. Whoever hears the origin of Goddess Hingulā, upon him **Chaṇḍikā Devī** is well pleased.

"Thus I have told you the Māhātmya of Hingulā. What now do you wish to hear?"

*Thus ends the sixty-sixth chapter, "The Origin of Hingulā, continued", of the Shrī Hingulapurāṇa within the Skanda Purāṇa.*

## Adhyāy 67: The Glory of the Hingul-tīrtha (હિંગુલતીર્થ માહાત્મ્ય)
*Printed pp. 213–214 (PDF 219–220)*

**The sages said:** How did the sage Nārada hear [this] from the mouth of Nārāyaṇa? Tell us all of it, if you have compassion upon us.

**Sūta-Purāṇi said:** Once the sage **Nārada**, wandering through all the worlds for the welfare of beings, came to **Satyaloka**. There, [in the company of Brahmā,] Nārada questioned **Nārāyaṇa**. **Nārada said:** "O God of gods, O Lord of the world, you are merciful to your devotees. How do men, drowning in [the ocean of] saṃsāra, reach the far shore? [Tell me,] for your account [is what redeems them]." **Nārāyaṇa said:** "O sage, I shall tell you the glory of the tīrtha, listen. There, where [the sacred place] is, **by its mere darshan all sin is destroyed**." **Nārada said:** "Tell me in full how that holy place came to be."

**Vishnu said:** "In that place the gods dwell; there abides the Goddess who destroys all sin; that place is most sacred. In this **Bhāratavarṣa**, this region, the **Daṇḍakāraṇya** and its inner reaches, is the more holy, and that place is mine. In the **Sahyādri** many holy places are found, [where siddhas and the like, and Vyāsa, dwell], and jewels of many kinds. There stand the **Sahyādri and the Vindhyāchala**; in that place the avatāras of Brahmā, Vishnu and Rudra were honoured. Among the rishis of the Sahyādri is the **Rāma-āshrama**, a place of liberation. This forest-hermitage, [from the Himālaya to the Sahya,] is most holy, and the devout come to it. From it **three sacred rivers** flow, the **Margathī, the Sombhram, and the Giril**. There is the great tīrtha named **Hingulā (Hindulā)**, where Hingulāsura arose; and by **bathing in this tīrtha a man is freed of all sin**. This tīrtha is most excellent, frequented by gods and rishis, [and its like is not found elsewhere] on earth. To make pilgrimage and bathe here in the month of **Māgha** is [supremely] meritorious; bathing in the Hingulā tīrtha [bears a fruit like that of the Gaṅgā], and as bathing in the [Sindhu waters] bears fruit, so bathing here destroys all sin. **As the tīrtha of Kāshi removes the sins of all, so too this place destroys all sin.** The merit one might gather over a thousand births is gained by a single act of worship at this place. Here **Bhārgava** [worshipped], and here **Hingulā made her appearance (prādurbhāva)**; and the most holy tīrtha named **Hindulamatī** is there."

**Sūta-Purāṇi said:** Hearing this, the sage Nārada rested a while on the bank of the **Giril**, and Nārada and all the rishis bathed in the Giril river, gaining the merit of bathing in that tīrtha. **Rāma and Lakṣmaṇa, and the sage Vālmīki[?]**, also [frequented] it. In this tīrtha all manner of sins are destroyed.

*Thus ends the sixty-seventh chapter, "The Glory of the Hingul-tīrtha", of the Shrī Hingulapurāṇa within the Skanda Purāṇa.*

> **Flags:** the **three river names** (Margathī · Sombhram · Giril) and **Vālmīki** are read tentatively from a worn page (see SCAN-QUALITY-NOTES.md, Priority 2). "Giril" is used both for a river and, elsewhere, for the sacred site, confirm on re-scan.

## Adhyāy 68: The Origin of the Hingulā and Mandākinī Tīrthas (હિંગુલા તથા મંદાકિની તીર્થોની ઉત્પત્તિ)
*Printed pp. 215–217 (PDF 221–223)*

*Sūta said:* The great-souled **Shrī Rāma**, with **Sītā** and **Lakṣmaṇa**, having gone there, [resolved] rightly upon the slaying of **Rāvaṇa**.

**Nārada said:** "How did the holy **Hingulā** and **Mandākinī** come to be? O protector of those who take refuge, tell me." **Lord Vishnu said:** Brahmā, who dwells in Brahmaloka, held in his **kalasha** the sin-cleansing water, which destroys crores of sins, water holy to the rishis, the ancestors and the gods. Taking this water from his **kumbha** for bathing, Brahmā went to the **Rāma-kṣetra** named **Hingulā**. In the forest of that kṣetra a sage ever meditates, where the munis recite the Vedas and the gods who receive the yajna-share dwell, where the stainless divine Brahman [is sought], a place set apart as if from Brahmā himself; dwelling there, when the body falls a man abides in **Vaikuṇṭha**. There **Rāma, Sītā and Saumitri (Lakṣmaṇa)** dwelt. In that Rāma-kṣetra, Brahmā, with the gods and the sages led by Anaka, took to austerity and performed tapas there for a full year.

At that time the fearsome demons **Madhu and Kaiṭabha**, grown vast, rose up to slay Brahmā. The whole earth with its mountains trembled as the demons drew near. In the tumult Brahmā's kumbha-water was poured out upon the earth and split into two parts. The water that went to the north became the river known as **Hindulā (Hingulā)**, and that which went to the south became the other. Thus arose those great rivers, destroyers of grievous sin. From the water of Brahmā's kamaṇḍal arose the rivers named **Girilā-kaṇṭha and Hindulā**. At their **confluence (saṅgam)** bathing yields great merit: even the sin of brahmahatyā is removed, and one is not reborn. At the confluence of these two rivers stands the **Rāmeshwar liṅga**, which Rāma installed; its mere darshan undoes all sin. Having fought five thousand years, Vishnu slew the demon-lord; then Brahmā returned with the rest, and the rishis went to their abodes. From the marrow (meda) of these demons the earth was drenched, and so the place came to be called [Mahiṣī]. Thus arose the Girilā tīrtha, which destroys sins of every kind and grants prosperity of every kind.

On the bank of the **Hindulamatī** lies the **Nāga-tīrth**, where a person is freed of any disease; no other tīrtha equals the Nāga-tīrth, the remover of all sins, and all the rishis frequent it. At that time a sinful, leprous man, who had once been a king and was now ruined, tormented by his disease and wandering the earth, came near **Hindulā**. He came to a muni and took refuge with him. The man said: "Wretched, I have come to your refuge. Protect me; in your mercy free me from all sin; tell me a means by which my affliction may depart." **Sūta-Purāṇi said:** Moved with compassion, the rishi told the king a means of solace. **The rishi said:** "I know one means: on the bank of the **Hindulavatī** is the **Nāga-tīrth**; go and bathe there, and you will be freed of leprosy." **Sūta-Purāṇi said:** He went to the Nāga-tīrth on the bank of the **Giril** and bathed, and all his sins were destroyed; he was freed of every disease and took on a divine form. Having enjoyed many pleasures in the world, at the last he went to **Swarga**. Thus on the bank of the Hindulā-Giril is the Nāga-tīrth, the Giril that destroys the mass of sins, frequented by the gods and rishis, which men should ever resort to.

*Thus ends the sixty-eighth chapter, "The Origin of the Hingulā and Mandākinī Tīrthas," of the Shrī Hingulapurāṇa within the Skanda Purāṇa.*

> **Flags:** river names read tentatively, and used interchangeably on the worn page (Hindulā/Hingulā, **Girilā-kaṇṭha**, Mandākinī, Hindulamatī/Hindulavatī); the place-name from the demons' marrow [Mahiṣī] and the sheltering muni's name are uncertain. Verify on re-scan (Priority 2).

## Adhyāy 69: The Nature of Mokṣa (મોક્ષનું સ્વરૂપ)
*Printed pp. 217–219 (PDF 223–225)*

*Sūta said:* The great-souled Shrī Rāma, with Sītā and Lakṣmaṇa, having gone there, [resolved] rightly upon the slaying of Rāvaṇa.

**Sūta-Purāṇi said:** Rāma, Lakṣmaṇa and Sītā came to this tīrtha, and from there resolved to slay **Rāvaṇa**. At the time when Rāma [worshipped] Hingulā and [sought] to destroy māyā, he asked his guru **Vasiṣṭha** about dharma. **Shrī Rāma said:** "O Guru, how can all people cross the ocean of saṃsāra? Tell me its means, O sage."

**Vasiṣṭha said:** "First I will tell you the nature of **māyā**. Men first take the non-self, the body and the rest, to be the self (ātma-buddhi); when this false notion arises through māyā, it is called saṃsāra. Through māyā this form is first imagined, with its coverings. This whole world is pervaded by māyā as a subtle covering, and apart from that the world stands wrapped in name and form. The ignorant are deluded, yet all is pervaded by the **Paramātmā**. On examination, the forms that appear, the seen and the manifold, are all unreal, like a dream. This is the root of the tree of saṃsāra, the bondage of rebirth.

The body is made of the gross elements and the five **tanmātras**; ahaṅkāra, buddhi, the senses and the mind, together with prakṛti, are all firmly bound up in it. The **jīva** is distinct from all of these; it is in truth the Paramātmā, ever called free. The jīva is realized through knowledge. Hear the means to that knowledge: control of mind, speech and body; the service of a true guru (**sadguru**); the abandoning of false refuges; truthfulness, the company of the good (satsaṅga), dispassion toward the objects of sense, freedom from egoism, the contemplation of birth, old age and death, devotion, equanimity in pain and pleasure, non-attachment, dwelling in a holy place, love of the Veda, bathing at the sacred fords, and the doing of all one's duties. One who does not do so remains in the state of ignorance.

This **ātmā** is formless, eternal and pure; it is without form, one and without a second (advitīya). To know the ātmā is **vidyā**, and by this knowledge one is merged in the Paramātmā. Thus I have told you the nature of **mokṣa**, which is most hard to attain; though a man has eyes, without this knowledge he does not truly see. Devotion to the Lord, faith in Īśvara, and love for him: these are all means of devotion. As your worship deepens, the grace of Girilā, though rare, is won. Near the **Sahyādri** this tīrtha is rare; it was brought up from Pātāla and set upon the earth."

Thus Vasiṣṭha resolved Rāma's doubt; the muni bowed, and Rāma went on to the river. So the Girilā, destroyer of sins, arose in the world from Brahmā, and at the confluence of these two rivers stands the most excellent **Hingul mountain**.

*Thus ends the sixty-ninth chapter, "The Nature of Mokṣa," of the Shrī Hingulapurāṇa within the Skanda Purāṇa.*
