# What is the Hinglaj Puran?

> An accessible explainer + provenance for the site's "About" / introduction. Reverent register, this is **itihaas (sacred history)**, revered as true; never to be framed as myth or fiction.

## In one line
The **Hinglaj Purān** is the sacred history of **Hingulā Mātā** (Hinglaj Mata), the account of how the supreme Goddess arose at Hingul, in the Sindhu land, and became the **kuldevi** of a people.

## What kind of text it is
It is a **sthala-purāṇa**: a scripture of a sacred place, declared to belong within the great **Skanda Purāṇa**. It does something quietly remarkable: it takes the universal Goddess of the pan-Indian tradition, the **Mahāshakti**, the power by which Brahmā creates, Vishnu preserves and Shiva destroys, and shows that she is one and the same as the Goddess of **Hingul**, on the Makran coast (in present-day Balochistan). The cosmic and the local are made a single living presence.

Its governing conviction, repeated across all 87 chapters, is simple and absolute: **Shakti is the ground of everything.** "Shiva without Shakti is inert." Every god, every cosmic act, even the great avatāras, are her doing, and that same supreme power is *your* mother, present at this mountain, for this community.

## How it is arranged
**87 adhyāy (chapters) in two pravāh (streams):**

- **Prathama Pravāh (ch 1–64)**: the rites of Goddess-worship and the **Devi Māhātmya**: the cosmogony, the forms of the Goddess, the Navrātri and Chaṇḍī observances, and the great accounts of **Madhu-Kaiṭabha** and the slaying of **Mahishāsura**: the buffalo-demon whom no god or man could kill, slain by the Goddess born of the gods' combined radiance (*tejas*).
- **Dvitīya Pravāh (ch 65–87)**: the **Hingula** account proper: the origin of Hingulā, the holy tīrthas of the Sindhu region, the campaign of **Parashurāma**, and the origins of the communities who hold Hingulā as their kuldevi.

## The book in your hands
This particular grantha is a **Gujarati translation of the Sanskrit original**, and a cultural artifact in its own right:

- **Translator:** Chhotalal Chandrashankar Shastri
- **Patron / publisher:** Hirji Prabhashankar, titled *Jyotirvidyābhūṣaṇ* (an honour in the science of jyotiṣa, whose hand shows in the grantha's chapter on auspicious time)
- **Press:** Mumbai-Gujarati Printing Press
- **Date:** **Samvat 1966 / CE 1915**: roughly **110 years old**
- **First edition; price at the time: Rs. 3**

## Why it must be preserved
Its value is not that it is a singular literary masterpiece, much of the first stream is the well-known Saptashatī material. Its value is that it is a **vanishing local recension**: a century-old rendering of a *sthala-purāṇa* that exists in few places and carries material found nowhere in the mainstream Devi Māhātmya, the Hingula-asura account, the geography of the Sindhu tīrthas, and the sacred origins of real communities. **If this book is lost, that specific weld of the universal Goddess to *this* place and *these* people goes with it.**

That is the mission of this project: **digital conservation**: turning every chapter into a faithful, readable archive entry and a visual representation, with the original text always readable and downloadable, so the grantha is carried whole to the next generation.
