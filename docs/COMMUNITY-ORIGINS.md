# The Community Origins: How Hingulā Became the Kuldevi

> The sacred history (itihaas) of how the **Brahmakṣatriya** and the lineages that branch from them, **Lohana, Bhāṭiā, Khatri, and the Pushkarṇā Brāhmins**: came to hold **Hingulā Mātā** as their clan-goddess. Drawn from Dvitīya Pravāh, ch 73–86. The standout content for the community this grantha belongs to.
>
> *Reverent register; some proper names in these chapters are read from a worn 1915 scan and await pandit review (see SCAN-QUALITY-NOTES.md).*

## The peril: Parashurāma's vow
The account begins in a fallen age. The sage **Jamadagni** keeps the wish-cow **Kāmadhenu**; the king of **Mahishmati**, **Kārtavīrya Arjuna (Sahasrārjuna)**, covets and seizes her, and in the strife Jamadagni is slain. His son **Parashurāma**, in grief and wrath, takes a terrible vow: to rid the earth of the Kshatriyas. By his borrowed power he sweeps the earth **niḥkṣatriya**: empty of the warrior caste, again and again. *(ch 73, 76–78)*

## The refuge: Hingulā shelters the survivors
The surviving princes flee. With the sage **Bharadvāja** and, above all, at the holy mountain of **Hingulā**, they take **sharaṇ** (refuge) and perform fierce austerities. And the Goddess **manifests** to them. *(ch 81–84)*

This is the hinge of the whole account. The prince **Jayasena** fears that to live disguised as a brahmin will invite a Kshatriya curse, *"your kshatriya-hood is destroyed; you are now brahma-kṣatriya."* The Goddess answers with her **vardān and her law** *(ch 84)*:

- worship me with your brothers, **as Rāma (Parashurāma) himself first worshipped here** at Hinguladri;
- **rule your city for a thousand years**;
- outwardly keep the **Vedic / brahmin rites** so that your line is established and safe;
- always honour the **Sāraswata Brāhmins**;
- and *"your lineage shall call me its **mother**: its **kuldevi**."*

Through the sage **Gautama** and the Sāraswata rishi, the princes receive their **mantra-dīkṣā**, and the surviving warriors become the **Brahmakṣatriya**: kshatriya in spirit, brahmin in observance, and forever devoted to Hingulā. *(ch 82–85)*

## The branching: how the communities arose *(ch 86)*
From this stock the communities branch, the grantha gives each a sacred origin:

- **Lohana**: when **Jayasena** ruled at **Kanauj**, his **forty-eight Rāṭhoḍ chieftains (sardārs)** fell into a crisis with the king; the Sāraswata brahmin **Durgadeva** mediated and, through devotion, made the king's rule secure. That chieftain-lineage took the name **Lohana**: from *loha*, iron, for the arms and iron-forts they had borne.
- **Pushkarṇā Brāhmins**: **Rāma (Parashurāma)** sanctified them as pure brahmins at the **Pushkar** lake near **Ajmer**; hence their name.
- **Bhāṭiā**: from the **Bhāṭi** Rajput line.
- **Khatri**: a trading branch of the same warrior stock.

All keep eternal devotion to Hingulā, having laid down the sword and taken to **commerce, trade and the scribal arts**: which is why a people once of the warrior caste are found, to this day, as merchants and administrators.

## Why this matters
This is not decoration in the grantha, it is a community's **charter of identity**. It answers, as sacred history, two living questions: *why do we, a warrior people, live as traders?* and *why is this particular Goddess of a distant mountain **ours***? The answer the grantha gives is that Hingulā herself decreed it, sheltering the survivors, naming herself their mother, and binding them to her by her own law.

Millions still keep **Hinglaj** as kuldevi on the strength of exactly this account. To preserve and tell it clearly, from the grantha's own words, is the single most valuable thing this archive can offer the community it belongs to.
