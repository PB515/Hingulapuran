# Hinglaj Puran — FAQ

> Accessible Q&A spun from the explainer, for the site. Reverent register — this is **itihaas (sacred history)**, revered as true; it is never to be framed as myth or fiction.

### What is the Hinglaj Puran?
It is the sacred history (*itihaas*) of **Hingulā Mātā** (Hinglaj Mata) — the account of how the supreme Goddess arose at Hingul, in the Sindhu land, and became the **kuldevi** of a people. It is a *sthala-purāṇa* (the scripture of a holy place), declared to belong within the great **Skanda Purāṇa**.

### Is this the same as the Devi Māhātmya / Durga Saptashatī?
Partly. Its **first stream (ch 1–64)** contains the well-known Devi Māhātmya material — the worship of the Goddess, and the great accounts of Madhu-Kaiṭabha and the slaying of **Mahishāsura**. But its **second stream (ch 65–87)** is unique: the **Hingula** account, the tīrthas of the Sindhu region, the Parashurāma campaign, and the origins of the communities who hold Hingulā as their goddess — material found nowhere in the mainstream text.

### Who is Hingulā / Hinglaj Mata?
She is the **Mahāshakti** — the supreme power by which Brahmā creates, Vishnu preserves and Shiva destroys — present at the mountain of Hingul. The grantha's whole conviction is that *"Shiva without Shakti is inert"*: she is the ground of everything, and at the same time the **mother (kuldevi)** of her devotees.

### Where is Hinglaj?
At **Hingul**, on the Makran coast — in present-day **Balochistan** (Hingol National Park, Lasbela). It is revered as one of the **Shakti Pīṭhas**. Her worship there is aniconic — a sacred presence rather than an idol — with the **akhaṇḍ jyot**, the eternal flame.

### How old is this book, and who made it?
This grantha is a **Gujarati translation** of the Sanskrit original, printed in **Saṃvat 1966 / CE 1915** — about **110 years old**. Translator: **Chhotalal Chandrashankar Shastri**; patron/publisher: **Hirji Prabhashankar** (titled *Jyotirvidyābhūṣaṇ*); press: the Mumbai-Gujarati Printing Press. First edition.

### How is it organised?
**87 chapters (adhyāy) in two streams (pravāh):** Stream 1 (ch 1–64) is Goddess-worship and the Devi Māhātmya; Stream 2 (ch 65–87) is the Hingula account. A full chapter list is in the **Anukramaṇikā (CHAPTER-INDEX)**.

### Whose kuldevi is Hingulā?
The grantha gives the sacred origin of the **Brahmakṣatriya** and the lineages that branch from them — **Lohana, Bhāṭiā, Khatri** and the **Pushkarṇā Brāhmins**. After Parashurāma's purge of the Kshatriyas, the survivors took refuge at Hingulā; she sheltered them, gave her law, and named herself their mother. (See the **Community Origins** piece and the **lineage diagram**.)

### Why does it matter — why preserve it?
Because it is a **vanishing local recension**: a century-old rendering of a *sthala-purāṇa* that survives in few places and carries material — the Hingula account, the tīrtha geography, the community origins — found nowhere else. If this book is lost, that specific weld of the universal Goddess to *this* place and *these* people goes with it. Carrying it whole to the next generation is the purpose of this archive.

### Is this "mythology"?
No. For the community it is **itihaas — sacred history, revered as true** — the account by which a people keep their goddess and their lineage. We hold it in that register throughout, and never as fiction.

### Are these translations final?
No — they are careful **working drafts**, made from a scanned century-old book, and marked where uncertain. A **pandit / native-scholar review is intended before any of it is published** as the community's own account.

### Can I read the original?
The mission is *"the original text always readable and downloadable."* The original Gujarati pages are themselves preserved as part of the archive, and a full English translation is underway alongside them.
